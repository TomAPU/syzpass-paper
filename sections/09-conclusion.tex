\section{Conclusion}
In this paper, we show that primitive discovery alone is an insufficient abstraction for Linux kernel exploit guidance: once an effect is selected as the primitive, the remaining effects on the same path determine whether that strategy is usable. We propose avoidance and neutralization as two strategies for reasoning about these effects and implement them in SyzPass, an automated framework for exploit primitive analysis and side-effect guidance. In a primitive-aware evaluation over 97 counted bugs, SyzPass reports 291 candidates under the pointer-materialization model, including 15 candidates under the portable object-matching model and 14 from 10 bugs that require side-effect neutralization. Finally, we develop three working kernel exploits following SyzPass guidance, covering object matching, path guidance, and constraint guidance.
