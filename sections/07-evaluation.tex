\section{Evaluation}
To evaluate SyzPass as exploit guidance, we formulate the two questions:

\noindent\textbf{RQ1. What primitive candidates and sensitivity results does SyzPass report under object matching alone and under pointer materialization?}
% How well does SyzPass facilitate exploit in general

\noindent\textbf{RQ2. What expert-assisted exploit evidence supports the reported guidance?}

To answer these research questions, we evaluate SyzPass on real-world vulnerabilities discovered by Syzbot. Details about the evaluation are illustrated in \S~\ref{expset}



\subsection{Experiment Setup}\label{expset}
% We evaluate SyzPass on 33 kernel bugs collected from Syzbot, dated between 2018 and 2024. These bugs span various kernel subsystems, including \texttt{net}, \texttt{bpf}, and \texttt{netfilter}, to ensure comprehensive coverage.

We collect our evaluation dataset from Syzbot by crawling kernel bugs reported between 2018 and 2025. We first exclude bugs without available PoCs, as our pipeline requires concrete inputs. Next, we retain memory-corruption reports from KASAN, ODEBUG, and general-protection-fault categories. We further prune unstable cases by automatically compiling and rerunning each PoC, excluding bugs that cannot be stably reproduced; this leaves 342 cases. Finally, we conduct symbolic execution for 4 hours on each case and keep the cases for which our symbolic executor reaches the end of the bug-triggering syscall or interrupt on at least one path. This leaves 115 memory-corruption bugs for subsequent analysis.

For each bug, we run context exploration for 10 hours; afterwards, we allocate 4 hours for primitive discovery and side effect avoidance. Side effects neutralization is performed without a time limit, as it completes deterministically.

\noindent\textbf{Threat model and accounting.} We separate three evidence buckets. \emph{OM-only candidates} are the portable baseline: the attacker can trigger the bug, control syscall and spray data, and reclaim vulnerable memory with sprayable objects, but residual effects must be made safe by object matching. \emph{PM-enabled candidates} additionally permit pointer materialization for AAW, CFH/FPD, and IF$_{sym}$ using address disclosure and kernel-addressable controlled memory; PM-additional sites are upper-bound sensitivity results, not SyzPass wins. \emph{Case-study evidence} is expert-assisted and records which SyzPass output was consumed by manual exploit development. Candidates are not completed exploits: allocator reachability, lifetime, KASLR disclosure, grooming, and final gadgets remain manual unless validated. A bug enters the aggregate when its record contains primitive-aware metadata for at least one counted site. Individual AVW/CVW sites without determined destination fields are excluded rather than inferred, because strict field-based matching cannot decide whether they target meaningful victim-object fields.

\noindent\textbf{Reading the counts.} The funnel is 342 stable bugs $\rightarrow$ 115 symbolically reachable bugs $\rightarrow$ 97 aggregate records $\rightarrow$ 781 counted primitive sites plus 522 unscored AVW/CVW sites. The 97-record aggregate is over analyzable explored paths, not a claim that the other stable Syzbot bugs lack candidates: 227 miss syscall/interrupt completion, and 18 more lack counted primitive-aware records. The 522 excluded AVW/CVW sites span 47 cases (283 AVW and 239 CVW). They are a trace-metadata limitation: the record identifies a value-write primitive but does not bind the write to a destination field, so Table~\ref{tab:primitive-aware-rules} cannot decide whether the write targets a useful victim-object field. We therefore report them next to the main result; if counted as failures, the OM-only rate is 15/1,303 and PM-enabled rate is 291/1,303, while SyzPass-attributed neutralization remains 14.

All experiments are conducted on Ubuntu 20.04.5 LTS with 2 TB memory and AMD EPYC 7542 32-Core Processor × 2 (total 64 cores).

\subsection{Case Studies}
To understand how SyzPass helps with exploit construction in practice, we conduct three case studies selected from locally triggerable bugs across different kernel versions. These case studies validate guidance produced by SyzPass rather than claim fully automatic exploit generation: SyzPass reports candidate primitives, paths, constraints, and matched objects, while the exploit developer still supplies steps such as choosing a KASLR bypass, heap-grooming method, and final ROP/gadget chain. We use \texttt{vulmem} to denote attacker-controlled OOB/UAF memory symbolized during analysis. All three case studies are working exploits; \texttt{380acd1} additionally validates a counted OM-neutralization candidate, while \texttt{e3cd8d} and \texttt{52bbc0} validate path and constraint guidance outside the strict OM-attributed aggregate.

For \texttt{e3cd8d}, SyzPass reports a CFH path guarded by \texttt{\_softexpires} and \texttt{state} constraints; the exploit consumes those heap-byte constraints, then manually supplies a KASLR source, \texttt{user\_key\_payload} spray, and final global-corruption payload. For \texttt{380acd1}, SyzPass reports an alternative \texttt{recvmsg()} trigger, an \texttt{MSG\_PEEK} path that avoids an unlink side effect, and a \texttt{msg\_msg} header match that neutralizes the unlink when avoidance is not used. We validated both strategies as KASLR-leak enablers and then completed privilege escalation manually through cross-cache placement and final control-flow hijack; this is the case that validates a counted OM-neutralization candidate. For \texttt{52bbc0}, SyzPass reports two PoCs and a CFH path whose constraints avoid an arbitrary-write side effect; the exploit consumes the constraint \texttt{vulmem[40:48] == 0}, then manually supplies a \texttt{user\_key\_payload} spray and ROP/global-corruption payload. These examples show that SyzPass can expose paths, constraints, and object matches consumed by expert exploit development, but they do not turn the aggregate candidates into automatically generated exploits.
