\section{Evaluation}
To evaluate SyzPass as exploit guidance, we formulate two questions:

\noindent\textbf{RQ1. What primitive candidates can SyzPass report under object matching and pointer materialization?}
% How well does SyzPass facilitate exploit in general

\noindent\textbf{RQ2. How does SyzPass guidance support exploit development?}

To answer these research questions, we evaluate SyzPass on real-world vulnerabilities discovered by Syzbot. We describe the setup in \S~\ref{expset}.



\subsection{Experiment Setup}\label{expset}
% We evaluate SyzPass on 33 kernel bugs collected from Syzbot, dated between 2018 and 2024. These bugs span various kernel subsystems, including \texttt{net}, \texttt{bpf}, and \texttt{netfilter}, to ensure comprehensive coverage.

We collect our evaluation dataset from Syzbot by crawling kernel bugs reported between 2018 and 2025. We first exclude bugs without available PoCs, as our pipeline requires concrete inputs. Next, we retain memory-corruption reports from KASAN, ODEBUG, and general-protection-fault categories. We further prune unstable cases by automatically compiling and rerunning each PoC, excluding bugs that cannot be stably reproduced; this leaves 342 cases. Finally, we conduct symbolic execution for 4 hours on each case and keep the cases for which our symbolic executor reaches the end of the bug-triggering syscall or interrupt on at least one path. This leaves 115 memory-corruption bugs for subsequent analysis.

For each bug, we run context exploration for 10 hours; afterwards, we allocate 4 hours for primitive discovery and side effect avoidance. Side effects neutralization is performed without a time limit, as it completes deterministically.

\noindent\textbf{Threat model and accounting.} We report two capability views. \emph{Object matching} (OM) is the portable baseline: the attacker can trigger the bug, control syscall and spray data, and reclaim vulnerable memory with sprayable objects, while residual effects must fit real object layouts. \emph{Pointer materialization} (PM) additionally permits address disclosure and attacker-controlled fake objects at known kernel addresses for pointer-shaped primitive or residual-effect requirements. PM captures stronger modern pointer capabilities, while the case studies show how reported paths, constraints, and object matches are consumed by exploit development.

\noindent\textbf{Counting terms.} A \emph{candidate site} is a primitive site with at least one explored path whose selected primitive is preserved and whose residual side effects are safe under OM or PM. A \emph{clean} candidate already has a safe path. A \emph{SyzPass-attributed} candidate becomes safe because SyzPass identifies guidance that resolves a residual side effect; in the OM aggregate below, these attributed candidates are object-matching neutralizations.

Overall, we observe 781 primitive sites across the 97 counted bugs. Under PM, 291 sites satisfy the candidate predicate, spanning 63 of 97 counted bugs and including 166 AAW, 83 CFH/FPD, 38 IF, and 4 CVW/AVW sites. This total consists of 276 PM-only pointer-materialization candidates plus the 15 candidates that also satisfy OM. The portable OM subset contains 1 clean site and 14 SyzPass-attributed candidates. This gap highlights how stronger pointer materialization changes the reachable primitive space while preserving object matching as the deployment-independent baseline.

For higher-risk primitives (OUW, AAW, CFH, and IF), pointer-shaped sites are preserved when their targets are controlled, while lethal pointer-shaped residual effects are handled by avoidance, object matching, or pointer materialization. The OM candidates are concentrated in double-free-like IF$_{con}$ sites whose residual effects require explicit object matching, showing why primitive-aware preservation and side-effect neutralization must be reasoned about together.

\subsection{Case Studies}
To understand how SyzPass helps with exploit construction in practice, we conduct three working exploit case studies selected from locally triggerable bugs across different kernel versions: \texttt{e3cd8d}~\cite{e3cd8d:online}, \texttt{380acd1}~\cite{380acd}, and \texttt{52bbc0}~\cite{52bbc}. These case studies focus on how SyzPass guidance is consumed during exploit development: SyzPass reports candidate primitives, paths, constraints, and matched objects, which guide later choices such as KASLR bypass, heap-grooming method, and final ROP/gadget chain. We use \texttt{vulmem} to denote attacker-controlled OOB/UAF memory symbolized during analysis. Together, the case studies cover three common uses of the report: choosing a safer trigger path, satisfying heap-byte constraints, and applying object-matching neutralization.

For \texttt{e3cd8d}, SyzPass reports a CFH path guarded by \texttt{\_softexpires} and \texttt{state} constraints; the exploit consumes those heap-byte constraints, then supplies a KASLR source, \texttt{user\_key\_payload} spray, and final global-corruption payload. For \texttt{380acd1}, SyzPass reports an alternative \texttt{recvmsg()} trigger, an \texttt{MSG\_PEEK} path that avoids an unlink side effect, and a \texttt{msg\_msg} header match that neutralizes the unlink when avoidance is not used. We validated both strategies as KASLR-leak enablers and then completed privilege escalation through cross-cache placement and final control-flow hijack; this case validates one counted OM-neutralization candidate. For \texttt{52bbc0}, SyzPass reports two PoCs and a CFH path whose constraints avoid an arbitrary-write side effect; the exploit consumes the constraint \texttt{vulmem[40:48] == 0}, then uses a \texttt{user\_key\_payload} spray and ROP/global-corruption payload. These examples show that SyzPass exposes paths, constraints, and object matches that are directly consumed during exploit construction.
